# Technical Deep Dive 09 – Simulation Realism vs Ethical Boundaries

This document explores the balance between realism and ethics
in internal phishing simulation programs.

The objective is to demonstrate mature security thinking beyond technical execution.

---

# 1️⃣ Problem Context

Phishing simulations must be realistic enough to:

- Accurately measure behavioural risk
- Identify awareness gaps
- Reflect real-world threat patterns

However, excessive realism may:

- Erode employee trust
- Cause emotional distress
- Disrupt business operations
- Introduce legal or reputational risk

The engineering challenge:

How realistic is “realistic enough”?

---

# 2️⃣ Dimensions of Realism

Realism can vary across multiple factors:

- Email tone (generic vs highly personalised)
- Domain similarity (obvious vs highly convincing)
- Urgency framing
- Internal branding usage
- Credential request simulation

Higher realism increases behavioural accuracy,
but also increases ethical and trust considerations.

---

# 3️⃣ Ethical Risk Spectrum

| Low Realism | Moderate Realism | High Realism |
|-------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Obvious training email | Convincing but controlled | Highly deceptive internal scenario |
| Minimal emotional trigger | Standard urgency | Emotional manipulation |
| Clear learning intent | Balanced training approach | Potential trust erosion |

Higher realism = higher reputational and cultural risk.

---

# 4️⃣ Design Trade-Offs

## Option 1 – Low Realism Simulation

Pros:
- Minimal backlash
- Low emotional impact
- Easier governance approval

Cons:
- May underestimate real-world risk
- Employees may not take it seriously

---

## Option 2 – Moderate Realism (Recommended)

Pros:
- Behavioural validity
- Balanced risk exposure
- Ethical alignment

Cons:
- Requires communication planning
- Requires executive sponsorship

---

## Option 3 – High Realism (Aggressive)

Pros:
- Maximum behavioural accuracy

Cons:
- Trust damage risk
- Potential HR/legal concerns
- Morale impact

---

# 5️⃣ Ethical Guardrails

A responsible phishing simulation should:

- Be formally authorised
- Avoid exploiting sensitive topics (e.g., health crises, personal tragedy)
- Avoid targeting individuals unfairly
- Avoid storing real credentials
- Include post-campaign education

Training must not become entrapment.

---

# 6️⃣ Organisational Trust Considerations

Security awareness is long-term cultural development.

If employees feel punished or embarrassed:

- Reporting culture may decline
- Trust in IT/security may decrease
- Defensive behaviours may increase

Balanced simulations improve culture;
over-aggressive ones damage it.

---

# 7️⃣ Transparency & Communication Strategy

Recommended approach:

- Executive sponsorship secured
- Clear policy documentation
- Post-campaign explanation
- Emphasis on learning, not blame
- Aggregated reporting preferred

Trust preservation is as important as risk measurement.

---

# 8️⃣ Legal & Compliance Considerations

Depending on jurisdiction:

- Employee privacy laws
- Data collection boundaries
- Internal policy requirements

Simulations must align with governance frameworks.

---

# 9️⃣ Engineering Reflection

Security maturity is not defined by:

“How deceptive can we be?”

It is defined by:

“How effectively can we improve resilience without harming trust?”

Phishing simulation is a cultural tool,
not an adversarial exercise.
